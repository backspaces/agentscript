Virus Model — Parameter Sweep

About this sweep

The model has 7 input parameters. A naive sweep of all of them would produce a grid too large to read and too hard to interpret. The challenge is identifying which parameters actually matter and how to reduce them to something comprehensible.

The key insight is that virusSpreadPercent and recoveryPercent are not independent — they don't have separate effects, they have a ratio effect. From SIR epidemic theory, what determines whether a virus spreads or dies out is the basic reproduction number R₀ = averageNodeDegree × spread / recovery. When R₀ < 1 the epidemic fizzles regardless of the individual values; when R₀ > 1 it takes off. So instead of sweeping two parameters and reading a 2D table, we collapse them into one derived metric and read a single curve.

This reduces the problem from "which of 7 parameters matter?" to two separable questions: (1) Does the epidemic spread? — answered by R₀ (spread and recovery combined); and (2) What does it leave behind? — answered by gainResistancePercent, the probability a recovered node gains permanent immunity rather than returning to susceptible. This parameter doesn't affect whether the virus spreads, only the resistant vs. susceptible split at the end.

The remaining parameters (population, initialOutbreakSize, virusCheckFrequency) either scale into R₀ or have weak effects on final percentages and are fixed at defaults.

The left chart plots resistant % vs R₀ on a log scale. Both curves follow a sigmoid: near zero below R₀ = 1, rising steeply through the phase transition, leveling off at high R₀. Higher gainResistancePercent consistently produces more lasting immunity.

The right chart plots ticks to completion vs R₀. Both peak sharply just above R₀ = 1 — where the virus spreads just fast enough to persist but not fast enough to burn through quickly. Low gainResistancePercent produces a much higher peak because recovered nodes cycle back to susceptible, giving the virus more fuel and extending the epidemic significantly.

Fixed: population = 150, averageNodeDegree = 6, initialOutbreakSize = 3, virusCheckFrequency = 1.

About the heatmap tables

Each cell shows the raw results for one specific combination of virusSpreadPercent (row) and recoveryPercent (column), averaged over all runs:

  • resist % — fraction of nodes that ended up permanently immune when the epidemic ended
  • susc % — fraction still susceptible (never infected, or recovered without gaining immunity)
  • ticks — steps until no infected nodes remained
  • R₀ — the derived reproduction number for that combo (averageNodeDegree × spread / recovery)

Cell color encodes resistant %: green = high resistance (epidemic burned through and left many immune nodes), red = low (virus fizzled early or left mostly susceptible nodes behind). The red cells in the top-right corner are low-R₀ scenarios where the virus barely spreads — resistant% is near zero not because the outcome was bad, but because almost nobody got infected in the first place.

The two tables show the same grid for each gainResistance value. Comparing them cell-by-cell shows where gainResistancePercent matters most: in the high-R₀ bottom-left corner, where the epidemic is guaranteed to spread and the only question is how much lasting immunity it leaves behind. In the low-R₀ top-right corner the two tables look nearly identical — gainResistancePercent is irrelevant when the virus barely takes off.

The charts above collapse these 25 cells per table into single curves by grouping cells that share the same R₀, making the overall trend easier to read.
